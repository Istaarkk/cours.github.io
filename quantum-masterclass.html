Chapter 01 — Bit vs Qubit

A qubit is an arrow on a sphere

A classical bit is 0 or 1. A qubit can be a smooth blend of both, called a superposition. Mathematically it's a 2-D unit vector with complex coefficients; visually it's a point on a unit sphere, the Bloch sphere.

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩   ,   |α|² + |β|² = 1

α and β are complex numbers. The squared magnitudes |α|² and |β|² are the probabilities you'd get 0 or 1 if you measured — which is why they must sum to 1. The north pole is |0⟩, the south pole is |1⟩, the equator is "50/50 with a phase". Drag θ and φ to glide between states. Note the live amplitudes: with θ=π/2, φ=0 you sit at |+⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 — perfectly balanced and the phase visible on the equator.

|0⟩ = [1, 0]ᵀ |1⟩ = [0, 1]ᵀ |+⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 |−⟩ = (|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2
Chapter 02 — Single-qubit gates as rotations

Every gate moves the arrow

A gate is a 2×2 unitary matrix. Multiply it onto your state vector and the arrow on the Bloch sphere rotates. That's the whole picture. Click a gate and watch.

X flips |0⟩↔|1⟩ (classical NOT). H turns |0⟩ into |+⟩ — it's how you create superposition. Z flips the sign of |1⟩, invisible if you only ever measure but crucial in interference. RY(θ) tilts the arrow by θ around the Y axis — that is the workhorse for "I want P(1)=p" (see Chapter 8). T = phase by π/4 (the gate names in the challenge are exactly these).

X = [[0,1],[1,0]]   H = (1/√2)·[[1,1],[1,−1]]   RY(θ) = [[cos(θ/2), −sin(θ/2)], [sin(θ/2), cos(θ/2)]]
Chapter 03 — More than one qubit

Tensor product : two qubits make a 4-vector

With n qubits the state lives in 2ⁿ dimensions. Two qubits → four basis states |00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩, four complex amplitudes, |α|²+...+|δ|²=1.

Each bar is the squared amplitude of one basis state — what you'd see measuring. Start from |00⟩ (only the first bar is full). Hadamard qubit 0: |0⟩→|+⟩ on q0, the full state is now |+⟩⊗|0⟩ = (|00⟩+|10⟩)/√2 — two equal bars. Apply H to q1 too: four equal bars, the "uniform superposition". This is the parallel-universe trick — every basis state is "alive" at once.

|ψ⟩ ⊗ |φ⟩ reads "the joint state of two independent qubits"   →  |00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩ basis
Chapter 04 — CNOT & entanglement

Two qubits with one fate

A CNOT (controlled-NOT) flips qubit B only if qubit A is |1⟩. Combined with a Hadamard, it makes the two qubits inseparable — measure one, the other's outcome is sealed. That's entanglement.

Start in |00⟩. Step 1, Hadamard qubit 0: the state is (|00⟩+|10⟩)/√2 — q0 is superposed, q1 still 0, independent. Step 2, CNOT: the |10⟩ branch flips q1 → the state becomes (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2, the Bell state. Now there are NO |01⟩ or |10⟩ bars. If you measure q0 and get 0, q1 is guaranteed 0; if q0 is 1, q1 is guaranteed 1 — even if they're light-years apart. The two qubits share a coin flip you can no longer separate.

CNOT = |0⟩⟨0| ⊗ I + |1⟩⟨1| ⊗ X
Chapter 05 — Measurement & the Born rule

Reading collapses. Repeat to learn.

Measuring picks one basis outcome with probability |amplitude|² and collapses the state to it. The trick: prepare the circuit many times (shots), measure each, build a histogram — the histogram approximates the probabilities.

Set the target probability of getting 1, choose how many shots to run, hit ▶. Each shot is one universe; the bar empirically rises toward |β|² = P(1). With 100 shots you get noisy noise; with 4000, the histogram lines up almost exactly with the true probability. "Shots = N, normalise = True" in the challenge's get_measure() is doing exactly this.

P(outcome k) = |⟨k|ψ⟩|²    — the Born rule, the only weird postulate of QM
Chapter 06 — The universal U gate

One gate to make any single-qubit state

Qiskit's u(θ, φ, λ) is the most general single-qubit gate. Applied to |0⟩, it gives you any point on the Bloch sphere. Memorise just one formula and you can produce any α, β you like.

u(θ, φ, λ) · |0⟩ = cos(θ/2)·|0⟩ + e^{iφ}·sin(θ/2)·|1⟩
 
(λ adds a phase on the |0⟩ branch — only relevant when you compose this gate with others)

The display shows the resulting amplitudes as |0⟩-bar and |1⟩-bar, with the |1⟩ amplitude split into real & imaginary parts so you literally see the phase e^{iφ}. Slide θ to control how much of the state is |1⟩; slide φ to spin its phase. Once you can dial a target [α, β] live, you'll know how to build any 1-qubit state the challenge asks for.

Chapter 07 — How circuits look in Qiskit

Three-line idioms you'll keep reusing

The notebook gives you a Circuit class (a subclass of QuantumCircuit). Here are the only patterns you actually need.

# a single qubit, starts in |0⟩
qc = Circuit(1)

# apply gates — order matters, left-to-right in time
qc.h(0)                  # Hadamard on qubit 0
qc.x(0)                  # X / NOT
qc.ry(theta, 0)         # RY by angle theta — your friend for P(1)
qc.u(theta, phi, lam, 0) # universal — any single-qubit state from |0⟩

# two-qubit gates
qc = Circuit(2)
qc.h(0); qc.cx(0,1)        # Bell state (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2

# peek at what the circuit produces — useful for sanity checks
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector
print(Statevector.from_instruction(qc).data)

# empirical histogram — what a real device would give you
print(qc.get_measure(shots=10000, normalise=True))
qc.ry(θ, q) the workhorse — controls how often you get 1
qc.u(θ, φ, λ, q) any single-qubit state including phase
qc.cx(c, t) CNOT (entanglement)
Statevector(qc).data exact [α, β, ...] — debug your circuit BEFORE measuring
qc.get_measure(shots=N) Born-rule histogram, the answer-checker
Chapter 08 — How to think about 6.1 and 6.2

The mental moves, not the answers

Strategy only. The numbers are yours — write them down, check with Statevector, submit when matching.

6.1 — make P(1) equal a chosen value p

Start from |0⟩. You want |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ with |β|² = p. The simplest gate that gives you a real β depending on a single knob is RY(θ). From the matrix in Chapter 02:

RY(θ)·|0⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + sin(θ/2)|1⟩

So β = sin(θ/2). Set sin²(θ/2) = p, solve for θ, plug it in, run get_measure(shots=10000) — your histogram of 1 should land near p. Don't be alarmed if it bounces by ±0.01% — that's the Born rule with a finite shot count.

Mini-exercise (do it on paper first)

Solve sin²(θ/2) = 1/2 for θ. (You should recognise this state.) Then sin²(θ/2) = 1. Then sin²(θ/2) = 0. Pattern check: at p=0, θ=0 — the gate is identity and you stay in |0⟩, exactly right.

6.2 — produce a state with a specific phase

Target: |ψ⟩ = (1/2)|0⟩ + (√3/2)·e^{iπ/3}|1⟩. Compare with Chapter 06's identity:

u(θ,φ,λ)·|0⟩ = cos(θ/2)·|0⟩ + e^{iφ}·sin(θ/2)·|1⟩

Two unknowns (θ, φ), two equations (real part of α, complex part of β). Read off θ from cos(θ/2) = 1/2, then φ is sitting in the exponent of the |1⟩ amplitude. λ can be anything because you start from |0⟩ — it disappears (try it in the U-gate slider). After you build the circuit, check with:

from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector
print(Statevector.from_instruction(q2_input).data)
# target: [0.5+0.j, 0.4330127+0.75j]

Mini-exercise

Without computing, predict what 0.4330127 is (it's the real part of β = (√3/2)·e^{iπ/3}). Useful: cos(π/3) = 1/2 and sin(π/3) = √3/2. You don't need anything beyond that.

Submission etiquette

The cell at the bottom of the notebook POSTs your two circuits encoded as base64 QPY. Run cells in order, verify with Statevector and get_measure first, then submit. If your probabilities are close to the target but the server rejects, re-run with more shots — Born-rule noise can put you on the wrong side of a threshold on a single attempt.

Chapter 09 — The two solutions, fully derived

From the target state to the line of code

Both circuits are one-liners. The work is in the algebra before you type them. Here is every step, every substitution, and why each constant lands where it does.

▸ 6.1  —  P(0)=33% , P(1)=66%

Step 1 — pick the gate. We start from |0⟩ and we want real amplitudes (no phase requirement, just a probability target). The only single-knob gate that produces a real β is RY(θ). From Chapter 02:

RY(θ)·|0⟩  =  cos(θ/2)·|0⟩  +  sin(θ/2)·|1⟩

Step 2 — match the Born rule. Probability of measuring |1⟩ is |β|² (Chapter 05). Here β is real, so:

P(1) = sin²(θ/2) = 0.66

Step 3 — solve for θ. Take the square root, then invert sine:

sin(θ/2) = √0.66   ⇒   θ/2 = arcsin(√0.66)   ⇒   θ = 2·arcsin(√0.66) ≈ 1.8909 rad ≈ 108.34°

Step 4 — sanity check the state. Plug θ back in:

α = cos(θ/2) = √0.34 ≈ 0.5831   ,   β = sin(θ/2) = √0.66 ≈ 0.8124
|α|² ≈ 0.34   ,   |β|² ≈ 0.66 ✓

Step 5 — the code.

from math import asin, sqrt

q1_input = Circuit(1)
q1_input.ry(2 * asin(sqrt(0.66)), 0)
# equivalent: q1_input.ry(2 * acos(sqrt(0.34)), 0)
# the notebook draft uses 0.33 instead of 0.34 — same angle to 2 decimals,
# fine because shot-noise covers ~±1% anyway

# verify before submitting
print(Statevector.from_instruction(q1_input).data)
# → [0.5831+0.j, 0.8124+0.j]
print(q1_input.get_measure(shots=10000, normalise=True))
# → {'0': ~0.34, '1': ~0.66}

Why RY and not RX?

RX(θ)|0⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ − i·sin(θ/2)|1⟩ — the |1⟩ amplitude is imaginary. Measurement probability is still sin²(θ/2), so RX would actually also work for 6.1 (Born rule only sees |β|²). RY is the textbook choice because it keeps everything real and matches the intuition "tilt around the Y axis on the Bloch sphere".

▸ 6.2  —  |ψ⟩ = ½·|0⟩ + (√3/2)·e^{iπ/3}·|1⟩

Step 1 — pick the gate. Now we need both a specific amplitude AND a specific phase. RY alone can't put a phase on β. The universal single-qubit gate u(θ, φ, λ) does exactly this in one shot (Chapter 06):

u(θ,φ,λ)·|0⟩  =  cos(θ/2)·|0⟩  +  e^{iφ}·sin(θ/2)·|1⟩

Step 2 — match the target term-by-term. Line up the two expressions:

cos(θ/2)·|0⟩ + e^{iφ}·sin(θ/2)·|1⟩  =  ½·|0⟩ + (√3/2)·e^{iπ/3}·|1⟩

Two scalar equations on the |0⟩ coefficient and the phase factor:

  • (a)   cos(θ/2) = 1/2  ⇒  θ/2 = π/3  ⇒  θ = 2π/3
  • (b)   e^{iφ} = e^{iπ/3}  ⇒  φ = π/3
  • (c)   sin(θ/2) = sin(π/3) = √3/2  — auto-satisfied once we fix θ from (a). This is the magic: the |1⟩ magnitude isn't a free parameter, it's locked to θ. We don't get to choose it independently; we get lucky because the target obeys |α|² + |β|² = (1/2)² + (√3/2)² = 1.

Step 3 — what about λ? Look at the full u-gate formula (the version Qiskit actually applies):

u(θ,φ,λ) = [[cos(θ/2), −e^{iλ}·sin(θ/2)], [e^{iφ}·sin(θ/2), e^{i(φ+λ)}·cos(θ/2)]]

λ only multiplies entries in the second column of u. When you act on |0⟩ = [1, 0]ᵀ, only the first column is read — λ vanishes. So λ can be anything (set it to 0). Try it in Chapter 06's slider: with θ and φ fixed, sliding λ moves nothing.

Step 4 — verify the complex β. Plug θ=2π/3, φ=π/3:

β = sin(π/3)·e^{iπ/3} = (√3/2)·(cos(π/3) + i·sin(π/3))
= (√3/2)·(1/2 + i·√3/2) = √3/4 + i·3/4 ≈ 0.4330127 + 0.75i ✓

That's the exact pair the notebook prints as the expected Statevector — [0.5 + 0.j, 0.4330127 + 0.75j].

Step 5 — the code.

from math import pi
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector

q2_input = Circuit(1)
q2_input.u(2*pi/3, pi/3, 0, 0)
#         θ=2π/3  φ=π/3  λ=0  qubit=0

print(Statevector.from_instruction(q2_input).data)
# → [0.5+0.j , 0.4330127+0.75j]   ← exactly the target

Alternative — RY then a phase gate

If the U gate feels too magical, decompose it: first set the magnitudes with RY, then stamp the phase with P (or RZ + global-phase bookkeeping):

q2_input = Circuit(1)
q2_input.ry(2*pi/3, 0)   # → (1/2)|0⟩ + (√3/2)|1⟩  (no phase yet)
q2_input.p(pi/3, 0)      # multiplies the |1⟩ branch by e^{iπ/3}
# identical statevector, three columns to read instead of one

Two valid mental models, same physics — pick whichever you'd be able to re-derive on a blank sheet of paper.

Why cos(θ/2) and not cos(θ)?

The factor of ½ in the angle comes from the Bloch sphere. A qubit on the Bloch sphere lives in projective 3-space; rotating the arrow by 2π brings it back to itself, but the underlying 2-D complex state vector picks up a sign flip and only returns after a 4π rotation. To compensate, every single-qubit rotation gate uses half the geometric angle. That's why θ=2π/3 puts the arrow at the equator-ish latitude where cos(π/3)=½ — the Bloch-sphere angle is twice the matrix angle.

▸ Final checklist before POSTing

  • q1_input — Statevector should give amplitudes ≈ [0.583, 0.812] (both real); get_measure(shots=10000) should land near {'0':0.34, '1':0.66}.
  • q2_input — Statevector must give exactly [0.5+0j, 0.4330127+0.75j]. If you see a different phase, you have φ wrong (sign or fraction of π).
  • Run the verification cells before the POST cell. The Born-rule noise can swing 6.1's histogram by ±1% on 10k shots — that's normal. 6.2 has zero noise on Statevector because it's computed exactly.
Chapter 10 — Challenge 2 · the gate-budget puzzle

Hit a 2-qubit target with a half-used circuit

The second notebook (J'optimise le porte à portes) gives you a circuit that already has H on both qubits, a strict budget of 5 gates with per-gate caps, and a 2-qubit target. The score is the fidelity between your output and the target — you must clear 95 %.

▸ What you're handed, what you owe

initial_circuit = Circuit(2)
initial_circuit.h(0)            # pre-applied H ⊗ H
initial_circuit.h(1)            # state after these two: |++⟩

target = Statevector([0.5, 0, 0, sqrt(3)/2])
#                       |00⟩    |01⟩  |10⟩    |11⟩
|ψcible⟩  =  ½·|00⟩  +  √3/2·|11⟩    (a biased Bell state — only |00⟩ and |11⟩ are populated, but unevenly)

Same axis as the Bell pair from Chapter 04, just with unequal weights. Born-rule outcomes: P(00)=¼, P(11)=¾, P(01)=P(10)=0 — q0 and q1 are still perfectly correlated, only the bias is non-Bell.

▸ Fidelity — the score the grader uses

F  =  |⟨ψcible|ψfinal⟩|²   ∈ [0, 1]

Take your output state, inner-product it against the target, square the magnitude. F = 1 means identical states (up to an invisible global phase). F = 0 means orthogonal. The baseline H⊗H circuit hits F ≈ 0.4665 — let's see why before we beat it:

⟨ψcible|++⟩ = ½·½ + 0·½ + 0·½ + √3/2·½ = (1+√3)/4 ≈ 0.683   ⇒   F = (0.683)² ≈ 0.4665 ✓

For pure states F is also the probability that, if the grader prepared |ψcible⟩ and measured it in your output's basis, the outcome would say "same state". F ≥ 0.95 means the two states agree on more than 95 % of every possible measurement.

▸ Read the budget like a sudoku grid

RY ≤ 2  — the only knob for splitting amplitudes (your friend)
RZ ≤ 1  — phase rotation (we won't need it; target is real)
X   ≤ 2  — classical NOT, swaps |0⟩↔|1⟩
CNOT ≤ 1  — your ONE chance at entanglement
total ≤ 5  — several slots stay empty if we're clever

No h, no u, no p in the menu — every Hadamard or phase tweak has to be synthesised from those four gates. The single CNOT is non-negotiable: the target is entangled (q0 and q1 are correlated), so without exactly one CNOT you can never leave product-state territory.

▸ Strategy — work backward from |00⟩

Phase 1. Pretend, for a moment, that you started from a clean |00⟩. How would you build the target with the gates available?

  1. RY(2π/3) on q0 sends |0⟩ → cos(π/3)|0⟩ + sin(π/3)|1⟩ = ½|0⟩ + √3/2|1⟩. State becomes (½|0⟩ + √3/2|1⟩) ⊗ |0⟩ = ½|00⟩ + √3/2|10⟩.
  2. CNOT(q0→q1) flips |10⟩ → |11⟩, leaves |00⟩ alone. Final: ½|00⟩ + √3/2|11⟩ ✓ exact target. Two gates, done.

Phase 2. But we don't start from |00⟩ — we start from H⊗H · |00⟩ = |++⟩. We have to undo the pre-applied Hadamards first. Since H isn't in the budget, we synthesise it from RY:

RY(−π/2)·|+⟩  =  |0⟩   ,   RY(+π/2)·|+⟩  =  |1⟩

Check: RY(θ) = [[cos(θ/2), −sin(θ/2)], [sin(θ/2), cos(θ/2)]]. Apply to |+⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩+|1⟩) at θ = −π/2: the new coefficients are ((cos(−π/4) − sin(−π/4))/√2, (sin(−π/4) + cos(−π/4))/√2) = ((1/√2 + 1/√2)/√2, (−1/√2 + 1/√2)/√2) = (1, 0) = |0⟩ ✓.

Naïvely that costs 2 RYs (one per qubit) just to un-do the H gates, on top of Phase 1's 2 gates = 4 gates total. Within budget — but we can squeeze better.

▸ The merge trick — same axis, add the angles

On q0 we plan two consecutive RY gates: RY(−π/2) (undo H) then RY(2π/3) (set the magnitude). Rotations around the same axis compose by simple angle addition:

RY(α) · RY(β)  =  RY(α + β)
RY(2π/3) · RY(−π/2)  =  RY(2π/3 − π/2)  =  RY(π/6)

Check on |+⟩: RY(π/6)|+⟩ with c=cos(π/12)≈0.9659, s=sin(π/12)≈0.2588 gives amplitudes ((c−s)/√2, (c+s)/√2) ≈ (0.5, 0.866) = (½, √3/2) ✓ — the exact magnitude split, in a single rotation. Two RYs collapsed into one.

On q1 there's nothing to compose with — it just needs to land on |0⟩ before the CNOT, so a lone RY(−π/2) finishes it.

▸ The 3-gate circuit, end to end

from math import pi, sqrt
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector, state_fidelity

optimized_circuit = Circuit(2)
optimized_circuit.ry( pi/6,  0)   # q0:  |+⟩ → ½|0⟩ + √3/2|1⟩
optimized_circuit.ry(-pi/2,  1)   # q1:  |+⟩ → |0⟩
optimized_circuit.cx( 0,    1)   # entangle      → ½|00⟩ + √3/2|11⟩

# the notebook composes initial_circuit (H⊗H) BEFORE your gates,
# so the full forward pass is:
#     H ⊗ H   →   RY(π/6) on q0   →   RY(-π/2) on q1   →   CNOT(0,1)
final_circuit = optimized_circuit.compose(initial_circuit, front=True)
print(Statevector.from_instruction(final_circuit).data)
# → [0.5+0.j, 0.+0.j, 0.+0.j, 0.8660254+0.j]   ← target, exactly

target = Statevector([0.5, 0, 0, sqrt(3)/2])
print(state_fidelity(Statevector.from_instruction(final_circuit), target))
# → 1.0   (perfect — the grader only needs 0.95)
3 gates used  — 2 of the 5-gate budget, hits the 2-RY cap
1 CNOT used  — the whole entanglement budget, but needed
0 RZ, 0 X  — target is real, no NOTs required
F = 1.0  — well above 0.95, plenty of headroom

▸ Sanity checks before POSTing

  • len(optimized_circuit.data) == 3 — over the per-gate caps and the grader will reject.
  • Statevector.from_instruction(final_circuit).data should print [0.5+0j, 0+0j, 0+0j, 0.8660254+0j]. The two middle zeros are the signature of a biased Bell state — any non-zero |01⟩ or |10⟩ amplitude means q0 and q1 drifted out of correlation.
  • state_fidelity(final, target) ≥ 0.95 — with the circuit above it's exactly 1.0, so even a small angle slip leaves headroom.

Why not slip an X in somewhere?

Tempting idea: keep q1 in |+⟩, then use X · CNOT to flip the populated branch… but X on |+⟩ is still |+⟩  (X·(|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 = (|1⟩+|0⟩)/√2 = |+⟩), so the gate is wasted. X only does useful work on basis states; on the equator of the Bloch sphere it's a permutation that often leaves the state invariant.

Could RZ help?

The target has real amplitudes — no phase to install. RZ would multiply |1⟩ by e^{iφ}, and fidelity (which squares the inner product, killing global phases) would still agree, but you'd burn one of your 5 gates for nothing. Skip it.

Alternative path — same answer, different route

If you forgot the merge trick, here's the unmerged version:

optimized_circuit.ry(-pi/2,  0)   # undo H on q0  → |+⟩ becomes |0⟩
optimized_circuit.ry(-pi/2,  1)   # undo H on q1  → |+⟩ becomes |0⟩
optimized_circuit.ry( 2*pi/3, 0)   # set magnitude on q0
optimized_circuit.cx( 0,    1)   # entangle
# 4 gates total — but 3 of them are RY, and the spec caps RY at 2.
# The grader will reject this even though F = 1.0.

The merge trick isn't an optimisation — it's what makes the solution legal. Read each cap as its own inequality: total ≤ 5 AND RY ≤ 2 AND CNOT ≤ 1, all simultaneously.

Try these — every one is a state you've already met

• θ=0  ⇒  pure |0⟩. β vanishes; slide φ_β all you want, it multiplies zero.
• θ=π  ⇒  pure |1⟩. Now φ_α multiplies zero. Only φ_β matters.
• θ=π/2, φ_α=0, φ_β=0  ⇒  |+⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2.
• θ=π/2, φ_α=0, φ_β=π  ⇒  |−⟩ = (|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2. Same magnitudes, opposite sign on β — phase π is what flips the sign.
• θ=π/2, φ_α=0, φ_β=π/2  ⇒  |+i⟩ = (|0⟩+i·|1⟩)/√2 — equal magnitudes but β picks up a pure imaginary phase.

The lesson, in one line

|0⟩ and |1⟩ never change. All quantum-circuit work — every gate, every algorithm — is just moving the α and β arrows around their respective complex planes while preserving |α|² + |β|² = 1.

About the 3D sphere above

The Bloch sphere is the geometric picture of the same state the two complex planes show analytically. The polar angle θ (latitude from the |0⟩ north pole) is exactly your magnitude slider. The azimuth Δφ = φ_β − φ_α (longitude around the equator) is the only phase that's physically observable — global phase shifts |ψ⟩ by an unobservable e^{iγ}, so the sphere only ever moves when the relative phase moves. How to read the mini-spheres

Each small sphere is the partial trace of one qubit — the state you'd see if you forgot the other one existed. For a separable state (e.g. |+⟩⊗|0⟩) both arrows sit on the surface, full length. For a Bell state both arrows collapse to the centre: each qubit alone is maximally mixed (50/50 with no usable phase), even though the joint state is perfectly known. The shorter the arrow, the more entangled the two qubits are. Entanglement is information stored in correlations, not in either qubit alone.

What biased Bell looks like

Run the default preset. You'll see two tall bars on |00⟩ (25 %) and |11⟩ (75 %), |01⟩ and |10⟩ empty, both partial-trace arrows sitting at z = −½ inside the spheres (the marginal P(1)=¾ pulls each arrow off the |0⟩ pole, the entanglement pulls it inward off the surface). The fidelity meter reads 1.0 because the bar pattern matches the Chapter 10 target exactly. Try replacing pi/6 with pi/3 and watch F drop — the bias is wrong.

Two-qubit gates worth exploring

• qc.cz(0, 1) — controlled-Z: same as CNOT but stamps a sign on |11⟩ instead of flipping the target. Symmetric in the two qubits — control and target are interchangeable.
• qc.swap(0, 1) — exchange the two qubits. On |01⟩ → |10⟩, but on the Bell state |Φ+⟩ it does nothing (Bell is symmetric).
• qc.cx(1, 0) — flip the roles. Same gate, different wiring; useful when q1 holds the "command bit" and q0 is what gets toggled.

Recap

The six sentences to memorise

01 A qubit is α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ with |α|² + |β|² = 1 — an arrow on a sphere.
02 Gates are unitary matrices; visually, rotations of that arrow.
03 n qubits live in 2ⁿ dimensions via the tensor product; CNOT entangles them.
04 Measurement gives outcome k with probability |⟨k|ψ⟩|², and collapses.
05 RY controls amplitude, U(θ,φ,λ) controls amplitude AND phase.
06 Same-axis rotations add their angles — RY(α)·RY(β)=RY(α+β) — which is how a 4-gate plan becomes a 3-gate one that passes per-gate caps. Score with fidelity F = |⟨target|ψ⟩|².
